Among the handful of written accounts of popular music in Ethiopia available to us, Francis Falceto delivered to readers a book entitled Abyssinia Swing: A Pictorial History of Modern Ethiopian Music(2001). Falceto's the thesis contains a comprehensive and concise assessment accompanied by historical images depicting the history of popular music and its development. The book is a standard reference for scholars and interested readers. Tesfaye Lemma is complex book (2013) titled Ethiopian of music history 1889-1993 (Ethiopian calendar).See more about Tesfaye Lemma He was one of the great personalities who spread Ethiopian music to the world and his work also contributed to the world of science. Tesfaye takes a closer look at both traditional and "modern" musical spheres. Tesafaye was a music composer, lyricist, choreographer (Sutton 2013) and above all a passionate cultural icon and ambassador. During the 1970s, he visited various Ethiopian ethnic groups in different areas of the country. His aim was to study the cultures, social environment and musical traditions of these communities. Thanks to this fieldwork, Tesfaye managed to collect many materials, mainly traditional musical instruments representing all four groups (aerophones, membranophones, chordophones and idiophones). Today, this huge collection of musical instruments is accessible at the Yared School of Music as a permanent exhibit. Today, Addis Ababa University's (AAU) Yared School of Music has preserved this rare collection of musical instruments

Ethiopian music is a term that can refer to any music of Ethiopian origin, but is often used for the genre, a distinct modal system that is pentatonic, with characteristically long intervals between some notes. The music of the Ethiopian highlands uses a basic modal system called qenet, which has four main modes: tezeta, bati, ambassador, and anchihoy. Three other modes are variations of the above: tezeta minor, bati major, and bati minor. When playing traditional instruments, these modes are generally not tempered (meaning the pitches may deviate slightly from the Western tuning system), but when playing Western instruments such as pianos and guitars, they are played using Western tuning system. The most prominent internationally recognized singers are Aster Aweke, Alemayehu Eshete, Gigi, Teddy Afro, Tilahun Gessesse and Mahmoud Ahmed. Tilahun Gessesse was popular throughout the 20th century and nicknamed "The Voice" because of his extended tenor vocals. In 2001, Teddy Afro debuted with his album Abugida and quickly became a famous singer of his generation, nicknamed "Blatenaw" (English: The boy). Some commentators have also compared Teddy Afro to Tilahun Gessesse in terms of musical style and patriotic sensibilities. Through her performances with leading Western jazz musicians such as Bill Laswell (who is also her husband) and Herbie Hancock, Gigi brought Ethiopian music to popular attention, especially in the United States, where she now lives. Through her 1999 album Hagere and 'Abebayesh', Aster Aweke is known for her voice which has attracted a wider audience. Another notable singer is Neway Debebe who was very popular among the youth in the 1980s and early 1990s with such songs as “Yetekemt Abeba”, “Metekatun Ateye”, “Safsaf” and “Gedam” among others. Abatte Barihun exemplifies all four major qenets on his 2005 album Ras Deshen.

Recent years, new music genres have been introduced, popular in Western countries, such as EDM, rock and hip hop. Musicians such as the Jano Band play a new style of progressive rock music with a mix of Ethiopian music.[16] Hip hop music began to influence Ethiopian music in the early to mid-2000s, culminating in the creation of Ethiopian hip hop, rhyming in the native Amharic language. The earliest and most influential hip hop rappers were Teddy Yo and Lij Michael, with the latter being more commercially successful. The success of both Jano Band and Lij Michael led to their inclusion in the 2017 edition of Coke Studio Africa[16] Electronic dance music in Ethiopia was not fully developed until the mid-2010s, although some use of electronic music with a hip hop element began in the year 2000. In 2018, a DJ and musician named Rophann introduced his own version of electronic dance music to the country, winning Album of the Year and further changing the mainstream music scene.
